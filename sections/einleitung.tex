\subsection{Background}

With the increasing adoption of multi-cloud and hybrid-cloud strategies, the need for consistent governance, security, and operating models is growing. In this context, the term \emph{landing zone} has become established as a designation for a prepared and controlled starting environment on which workloads can be deployed in a standardized way. The relevance of such starting environments results not only from the technical complexity of distributed cloud landscapes, but also from the need to harmonize policies, identities, networks, logging, and responsibilities across platforms.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Although the term is widely used in practice, it is not defined uniformly by cloud providers. Some descriptions mainly emphasize multi-account structures and governance, while others focus on modular reference architectures, security controls, or reusable \ac{iac} building blocks. This makes direct comparison difficult, especially when a technical, cross-provider definition is required for architectural or research purposes.

\subsection{Aim and Research Question}

This report examines how major cloud providers conceptualize landing zones, which similarities and differences their models show, and which provider-independent definition can be derived from them. This leads to the following research question: \textit{How do landing zone concepts differ across major cloud providers, and what common definition can be derived from their similarities and differences?}

\subsection{Scope and Structure}

The focus is not on implementation details of individual services, but on the conceptual level. Azure, AWS, Google Cloud, Alibaba Cloud, and IBM Cloud are examined as representative enterprise-oriented platforms. The report first clarifies the conceptual and technical background, then explains the methodology, followed by the comparative analysis. Finally, the findings are discussed and a condensed answer to the research question is provided.
