\subsection{Answer to the Research Question}

Landing zone concepts differ across major cloud providers mainly in terminology, structural model, and proximity to implementation. Azure, AWS, Google Cloud, and Alibaba Cloud largely understand landing zones as organization-wide governance and operating foundations, while IBM Cloud frames the term more strongly around deployable, secure infrastructure foundations. However, all concepts share the idea of providing a standardized starting environment in which security, governance, resource organization, and automation are defined before broad workload rollout.

\subsection{Summary of Main Findings}

The main similarities identified are the role of landing zones as a foundation for controlled cloud adoption, the focus on security and governance guardrails, and the strong role of automation. The main differences concern resource hierarchy, the type of policy inheritance, the scope of the organizational framework, and the proximity to concrete product artifacts. These findings lead to a robust provider-independent definition of a landing zone as a standardized, scalable, and automated starting environment for controlled cloud operation.

\subsection{Outlook}

A useful continuation of this work would be, first, to extend the comparison to additional providers or managed platform variants and, second, to validate the proposed definition empirically in real enterprise environments. It would be particularly relevant to examine how far the derived definition proves practically useful in multi-cloud operating models, heavily regulated industries, and provider-specific platform engineering teams.
